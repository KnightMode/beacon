## Design Context

### Users
Beacon should persuade engineering leads and platform teams who need codebase intelligence to work in Slack without losing evidence, permissions posture, or operational control. They are evaluating Slack-native AI workflows for answering code questions, reviewing PRs, triaging CI, and turning thread context into fixes.

### Brand Personality
Sharp, credible, and evidence-first. The interface should feel like an engineering war room: fast, precise, skeptical of vague AI claims, and confident enough to show its citations.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use an "evidence war room" visual language: split-screen dossiers, command rails, annotated Slack transcripts, retrieval traces, and proof tags. Avoid generic dark neon AI, bland card grids, and decorative lighthouse metaphors unless they directly support the Beacon name. The market contrast should be explicit against Cursor Slack Agent and CodeRabbit-style Slack agents.

### Design Principles

1. Lead with proof, not possibility: every major claim should be tied to citations, retrieval, code graph context, or CI evidence.
2. Sell the loop: Beacon is not just chat, review, or triage; it connects Slack questions, code evidence, PR review, CI diagnosis, and fix PR creation.
3. Position against current options directly: show where editor-first agents and review-only bots stop, then show where Beacon keeps going.
4. Make Slack feel like the control room: the product should look native to the engineering thread while still feeling purpose-built and premium.
5. Prefer sharp editorial hierarchy, dense but readable artifacts, and asymmetric layouts over generic feature cards.
